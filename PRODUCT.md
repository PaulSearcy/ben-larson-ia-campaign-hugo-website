# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Persuadable voters in Iowa House District 19 who don't know Benjamin Larson yet. They're browsing to evaluate candidates before an election—often skeptical, short on time, and comparing options. They need to quickly determine: Is this person credible? Do they understand my concerns? Can they actually get things done?

## Product Purpose

Convert unfamiliar voters into supporters by establishing Ben as a trustworthy, competent, working-class voice for Iowa families. Success means a visitor leaves feeling they understand who Ben is, what he stands for, and that he's someone who will genuinely represent their interests—not a politician playing a role.

## Brand Personality

Working-class, Direct, Authentic. Ben is a cardiac care nurse who started as a 20-year-old paramedic in rural Nebraska. He lost his father to cancer at 16. He organized for worker safety, stood with farmers during pipeline protests, and worked ICU during COVID. This isn't a career politician—it's someone who has lived the problems he's running to fix. The voice is plain-spoken, not polished; confident, not slick; rooted, not aspirational.

## Anti-references

- **Slick DC consultant campaigns**: Stock handshake photos, focus-grouped messaging, "I approved this message" energy. Feels bought, not earned.
- **Boring government portals**: Institutional gray, walls of text, dry and forgettable. Communicates bureaucracy, not urgency.
- **Tech startup aesthetics**: Gradient blobs, "disrupt" language, San Francisco minimalism. Wrong vocabulary for a working-class nurse running in Iowa.
- **Navy-and-gold Democratic template**: The default campaign color scheme is so overused it's invisible. Differentiate or disappear.

## Design Principles

1. **Show the real person.** Ben's biography—rural Nebraska, paramedic at 20, ICU during COVID, cardiac nurse today—is his differentiation. Lead with authentic photography and specific stories, not generic campaign imagery.

2. **Earn trust through substance.** Persuadable voters are skeptical. Concrete policy positions and real experience matter more than slogans. Don't ask for trust—demonstrate why it's warranted.

3. **Speak plainly.** Working-class authenticity means no consultant-speak, no jargon, no hedge words. Say what you mean. If a sentence sounds like it came from a focus group, rewrite it.

4. **Root in place.** This is Iowa House District 19—Council Bluffs, working families, the Missouri River. The design should feel Midwestern and grounded, not imported from DC or the coasts.

5. **Respect the voter.** Accessible, fast, no tricks. Treat visitors as intelligent adults making a serious decision about their representation.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA compliance. Good contrast ratios (especially for body text), full keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and reduced-motion alternatives for any animations. Iowa has an aging population—ensure the site works well for visitors with vision or motor impairments.
